The perceptual ambiguity of in-door scenes

(You can press the SPACEBAR to end the experiment)

 

The experiment you just completed is about the general topic of perception and memory of scenes. The specific topic here is the perceptual ambiguity of discrete scene categories. How your perception of scene categories influenced by the stimuli presented when there is ambiguity? Hysteresis is an interesting effect found in perceptual ambiguities. It means the dependence of the previous state in a system. In psychophysics, hysteresis refers to the phenomenon that the perception of stimuli is influenced by prior stimuli. People tend to stick with the perception brought by previous stimuli until the later stimuli propose a large enough change in perception. Therefore, in categorical judgements, the threshold of perception on category change can depend on the direction of presenting the stimuli. Hysteresis effect has been found in many studies, such as geometric and emotional ambiguities, figure-ground transitions, linguistic ambiguities, and so on. Here we are interested to see if we can also find the hysteresis effect in judgements of scene categories, and how this gives inferences on scene perception.

In the experiment, you completed two parts of experiment. In part A of the experiment, you were presented with image sequences, and your task was to decide a change in terms of category. All transitions between categories were presented to you in both directions, and we will see your perceptions of changes in categories differ on the same image sequence, but presented in opposite directions. We hypothesize that your judgement of change in categories will be different in two directions, as, in each direction, you tend to stick longer to the previous category you saw. In part B of the experiment, we asked you to respond to immediate changes, to collect your response time. This response time will be extracted from the data we collected from part A, so the effect of response time can be eliminated.

This experiment will inform us on how scene perception is similar or different from other perceptions. It can also give implications on the experiment paradigms including presenting stimuli in a specific order, and how the order of presenting may influence the results.
